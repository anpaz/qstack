\section{Conclusion and Future Work}\label{sec:conclusion}

In this paper, we have presented qstack, a novel quantum programming framework that fundamentally rethinks how quantum and classical computation interact. Our stack-based approach, with its emphasis on dynamic qubit allocation, callback-based classical control, and modular execution, addresses several critical challenges in quantum programming language design, particularly those related to quantum error correction and hardware-independent compilation.

Looking ahead, several exciting research directions could further enhance qstack's capabilities. Compiler correctness represents perhaps the most promising frontier: qstack's multi-stage compilation framework, with its sequence of transformations across different abstraction levels, creates new opportunities for proving the correctness of these transformations. The clean separation between quantum execution and classical callbacks simplifies reasoning about program transformations, potentially enabling formal guarantees that were previously infeasible with more tightly coupled models. Additionally, formal verification of quantum programs themselves—distinct from compiler correctness—could leverage qstack's structured approach to quantum-classical integration to prove properties of hybrid quantum-classical algorithms. Automatic fidelity calculations could complement this by enabling simulation of logical gates at higher abstraction levels through derivation of effective noise models from physical-level simulations, significantly improving the efficiency of large-scale fault-tolerant algorithm simulation. Integration with specialized error correction frameworks like Stim would further leverage optimized stabilizer circuit simulation and sophisticated error analysis tools while maintaining qstack's high-level programming model. Additionally, incorporating hardware-specific constraints and optimizations—such as qubit connectivity, gate fidelities, and coherence times—would enable qstack to automatically transform abstract quantum programs into implementations tailored to specific quantum processors.
